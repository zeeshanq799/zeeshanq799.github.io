AdSense Page RPM vs. Impression CPM

Many publishers get confused by the different "Rates" shown in AdSense. In 2026, Google has shifted more towards pay-per-impression models, but the distinction remains critical:

Page RPM vs Impression CPM Comparison
Metric What it Measures Why it Matters
Page RPM Earnings per 1,000 pageviews Holistic view of site efficiency
Impression CPM Cost per 1,000 ad impressions Valuation of individual ad slots
Query RPM Earnings per 1,000 searches Efficiency of Search components

If your Impression CPM is high but your Page RPM is low, you might not be showing enough ads per page. Conversely, if your Page RPM is high but CPM is low, you have massive traffic volume but low ad value. Aim to optimize for Page RPM as it accounts for the entire user session revenue.

How Does Website Monetization Work?

For bloggers and publishers, Google AdSense (or alternatives like Mediavine/Raptive) is the primary way to earn money. Revenue is driven by two main models:

  • CPC (Cost Per Click): You get paid when a user clicks an ad. This is calculated using the formula: Traffic √ó CTR √ó CPC.
  • CPM (Cost Per Mille): You get paid just for showing the ad (per 1,000 views). This is common for "Brand Awareness" campaigns (similar to YouTube CPM).

3 Ways to Increase Your Ad Income

  1. Improve Viewability: Place ads "Above the Fold" (visible without scrolling). Ads that are actually seen have much higher CTRs.
  2. Focus on High-Intent Keywords: Instead of writing "What is a laptop?", write "Best laptops for students in 2026." The second keyword signals the user wants to buy something, attracting higher-paying ads.
  3. Optimize Site Speed: Ads load faster on fast websites. If your site takes 5 seconds to load, users might scroll past the ad slot before the banner even appears.
