Many creators confuse CPM and RPM.

  • RPM: What YOU earn per 1,000 views after YouTube‚Äôs cut

YouTube typically keeps 45% of ad revenue. So a $20 CPM does NOT mean you earn $20.

Frequently Asked Questions About YouTube Pay Per 1,000 Views

How much does YouTube pay per 1,000 views in 2026?

In 2026, YouTube typically pays creators between $2 and $12 per 1,000 views based on RPM (Revenue Per Mille). Earnings can range from $1 to $25+ depending on niche, audience country, watch time, and advertiser demand. Finance and business channels usually earn the highest RPM.

How much does YouTube pay per 1,000 views in the USA?

YouTube creators with a United States audience generally earn between $8 and $20+ per 1,000 views. Finance and investing channels often exceed $15 RPM due to strong advertiser competition in the US market. Earnings depend on niche, watch time, ad placement, and audience engagement quality.

How much does YouTube pay for 1 million views?

Earnings for 1 million views depend entirely on RPM. At a $3 RPM, creators earn around $3,000. At a $10 RPM, earnings reach $10,000. High-RPM finance or business channels can generate $15,000 or more for 1 million views, especially with a Tier 1 audience like the USA or UK.

What is the difference between RPM and CPM on YouTube?

CPM (Cost Per Mille) is what advertisers pay per 1,000 ad impressions, while RPM (Revenue Per Mille) is what creators actually earn per 1,000 views after YouTube‚Äôs revenue share. RPM is always lower than CPM because YouTube keeps approximately 45 percent of advertising revenue before paying creators.

Does YouTube pay the same for Shorts?

No. YouTube Shorts earnings are calculated through a shared revenue pool model and typically result in lower RPM compared to long-form videos. Shorts RPM often ranges from $0.02 to $0.20 depending on region, advertiser demand, and total Shorts performance within the revenue pool.

What affects how much YouTube pays per 1,000 views?

YouTube earnings per 1,000 views are influenced by niche, audience country, watch time, advertiser competition, video length, number of mid-roll ads, and overall engagement rate. Tier 1 audiences such as the USA, UK, Canada, and Australia typically generate higher RPM compared to developing markets. See our detailed breakdown of YouTube RPM by country for more insights.
